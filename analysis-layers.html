Three Layers of Analysis

The Smart Citizen ecosystem operates through interconnected layers that transform environmental data into community action

1

Ontological Analysis

What exists in the Smart Citizen ecosystem? What are its components, infrastructure, and modes of interaction?

  • A. Hardware Infrastructure
    • Smart Citizen Kit (SCK): Modular, open-source, low-cost environmental sensing device.
    • Core Modules:
      • Data Board: Wi‑Fi microcontroller with SD logging and expansion ports.
      • Sensor Boards: Urban Board (T, RH, pressure, light, noise, UV) and optional modules (PM2.5, NO₂, VOC, CO₂, water flow, gas sensors).
    • 3D-Printed Enclosures: Accessible, locally fabricated, with outdoor-ready options.
    • Power Management: USB, battery, or solar panel for decentralized installations.
  • B. Digital Infrastructure
    • Embedded Firmware: Controls data acquisition, timestamping, buffering, and transmission.
    • Smart Citizen Platform: Cloud-hosted dashboard for real-time/historical data, CSV export, RESTful API.
    • APIs & Developer Resources: Extensive API docs, integration with Python, Jupyter, R.
  • C. Human and Social Layer
    • Actors: Local citizens, community organizations, educators, policy advocates, researchers, developers.
    • Practices: Participatory sensing, collective analysis, calibration workshops, advocacy, storytelling.
  • D. Knowledge & Documentation
    • Extensive technical docs, tutorials, circuit diagrams, CAD files, metadata standards.
    • Online knowledge base: Sensing principles, sensor accuracy, deployment best practices.
2

Historical & Contextual Analysis

What was the context of its creation? What need did it respond to?

  • A. Historical Genesis
    • Originated in 2012 at Fab Lab Barcelona (IAAC).
    • Response to corporate-led smart city frameworks that excluded citizens from data ownership and decision-making.
    • Launched during the rise of open hardware, participatory sensing, and citizen science.
  • B. Philosophical Position
    • Counters techno-solutionism and top-down governance; promotes grassroots action.
    • Aligns with environmental justice, data commoning, and sovereignty—especially in urban stress contexts.
  • C. Intended Audiences
    • Primary: DIY citizen scientists, educators, activists, air quality watchdogs.
    • Secondary: Policymakers, urban planners, sustainability researchers.
  • D. Sociopolitical Conditions
    • Post-2008 crisis: citizen empowerment and democratic innovation in Europe.
    • Supported by EU-funded programs (Making Sense, iSCAPE, DECODE) for co-creation and shared infrastructure.
3

Visual & Aesthetic Representation

How does Smart Citizen communicate its ideas through visual and design language?

  • A. Visual Style and Communication
    • Functional, minimalist, schematic—rooted in maker movement and Fab Lab principles.
    • Dashboards: sensor icons, line graphs, color-coded status for clarity to all users.
    • 3D models and assembly diagrams: open-source, educational (exploded views, GitHub CAD releases).
  • B. Representation as Rhetoric
    • Visual simplicity = transparency and trust. Emphasis on legibility, hackability, open access.
    • Rawness of enclosure/wiring: tool to be understood, customized, iterated—not a black box.
  • C. Precedents and Influences
    • Arduino, Processing: open-source electronics and creative code.
    • Design Justice: accessibility and open design.
    • "Critical making": blending technical skill with cultural critique.
