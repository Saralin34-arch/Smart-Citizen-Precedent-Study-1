Smart Citizen Concept

Citizen Monitoring Sciences

Rooted in citizen monitoring sciences, Smart Citizen promotes open participation in environmental research and advocacy. The platform is guided by principles of co-creation, change-making, empowerment, and openness.

  • Monitoring of air, water, and biodiversity
  • Community-based action frameworks
  • Control over data access and privacy

B. Methodological Analysis

  • 1. Software Approach:
    • Built on Arduino-compatible microcontrollers
    • Firmware enables real-time data capture and network transmission (via Wi-Fi)
    • Modular code architecture enables swapping sensor types without rewriting the entire system
    • Platform is built around open RESTful APIs for data retrieval and real-time streaming
  • 2. Data Use + Provenance:
    • All data generated by citizen-deployed sensors
    • Data includes: Environmental variables (e.g. temperature, PM2.5, NO₂), Metadata (timestamp, location, sensor ID)
    • Transparency ensures reproducibility and accountability
    • Datasets are hosted openly with public CSV access or API pull (API example)
  • 3. Theoretical + Epistemological Grounding:
    • Inspired by citizen science, data justice, and critical making
    • Framework aligns with Donna Haraway’s “situated knowledge” – sensor data is local, contingent, embedded
    • Not just “data collection,” but a community-led inquiry process

C. Rhetorical Analysis

What is Smart Citizen trying to say?

  • “Environmental knowledge should not be monopolized.”
  • The project challenges centralized control of environmental data by empowering individuals and communities with tools to measure and understand their own surroundings.
  • Instead of passively consuming “smart” services, citizens become co-creators of urban knowledge.

How is this argument communicated?

  • Openness: All hardware designs, code, and data are public
  • Simplicity: UI is intuitive, educational, non-corporate
  • Participation: Workshops, community labs, citizen pilots are foregrounded in the documentation
  • Critique: It resists the dominant smart city paradigm of surveillance and commercial optimization

D. Fit Within Author’s Wider Practice

  • Smart Citizen is part of Fab Lab Barcelona's broader work in:
    • Open hardware for civic use
    • Citizen engagement in environmental governance
    • Decentralized infrastructures
    • European research projects like:
      • Making Sense: civic engagement through sensing
      • iSCAPE: air pollution reduction through behavioral change
      • DECODE: digital democracy and data commons
      • Reflow: urban material flows and circular economy
  • Guillem Camprodon, one of the project leads, also contributes to writing and speaking about urban technology democratization, open source governance, and citizen empowerment through tools.

E. Personal Assessment (Appraisal + Criticism)

  • Strengths:
    • Modular, user-configurable system adaptable to different issues (air, noise, water)
    • Deep commitment to openness—uncommon in the IoT/smart city sector
    • Strong community orientation, both digitally (GitHub/API) and locally (workshops)
  • Limitations:
    • Sensor accuracy is limited by cost and environmental variables (e.g. humidity affecting PM readings)
    • Calibration is time-consuming and not accessible to all users
    • Reliance on Wi-Fi and power infrastructure may exclude rural or resource-scarce deployments
  • Opportunities:
    • Create a low-tech version (e.g. LoRa or mesh-based kits)
    • Build a community dashboard for storytelling, not just data
    • Introduce real-time alerts for spikes in pollution or temperature

Critique: Smart Citizen walks the line between tool-making and advocacy. While its technical contributions are strong, it could further support policy translation—making sure the data gathered is legible to institutions and not just individuals.
